The first slot contains personal prefixes. The relative order of three tense-aspect-mood suffixes can be seen in the following example:

(ctorat-40)
tipapëjsejne waijtajne

Aspect

The pluractional marker -pëti.

This aspect-marking morpheme is known from other Cariban languages (Mattiola and Gildea under review). This example illustrates its -pëj allomorph:

(ctorat-40)
tipapëjsejne waijtajne
tipa-pëj-se-jne go.group-PLUR-PST-PL
waijta-jne mouse-PL
‘las ratas se fueron’

Tense

-jpë

-jpë is etymologically a nominalizer, but now it also functions as a simple past marker:

(anfoperso-02)
ana këyetajpë, intipijkë ana chi yawë
ana 1+3
këyeta-jpë grow.up-PST
intipijkë a.little
ana 1+3
chi-∅ COP-IPFV
yawë TEMP.LOC
‘nos criamos cuando estábamos chiquiticos nosotros’

It also occurs on nouns:

(anfoperso-17)
tawara ma ana këyetajpë, ana papa pan patajpë të
tawara too
ma-∅ throw-IPFV
ana 1+3
këyeta-jpë grow.up-PST
ana 1+3
papa father:VOC
pan deceased
pata-jpë town-PST
të-∅ go-IPFV
‘así nos criamos después de que se murió mi papá en su pueblo’

The supine

The suffix -se can be used to express a co-called supine meaning, i.e., purpose of motion. Intransitive verbs occur unprefixed, while transitive verbs can carry person prefixes.

(ctorat-03)
enijpëtërë waraijtokomo ijtëse ti mïjna tëijpo wïnïjse
enijpëtërë one
waraijtokomo man
ij-të-se 3-go-PST
ti QUOT
mïjna there
tëijpo far
wïnïj-se sleep-SUP
‘un hombre se fué allá, a dormir lejos’
